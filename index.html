Cancer Incidence Data Discovery

Which regions of the country are most prone to cancer?

  • Select a state to view mean cancer incidence per 100,000 people.
  • Highest levels appear in the Northeastern United States, with outliers including Kentucky, Illinois, and Louisiana.
  • This visualization also appears in the Cancer Incidence Group Project.

How do factors affecting incidence vary by region?

  • A state-level map of incidence rate by region alongside death rate. Larger indicators represent higher death rates.
  • The map generally shows higher death rates where incidence is higher, such as in NY and PA, with some outliers at mid-level incidence like Iowa and Michigan.
  • Two correlation views relate income and poverty to death and incidence rates. Texas stands out with both high poverty and high death rate, and also with high median income and high incidence rate.
  • In both correlations, the Northeast appears in the upper-right quadrant, indicating a strong relationship with these factors.
  • A bar chart provides five-year trends by state and recent changes for context and Texas indicated that the trends were falling.

New York State focus

  • The county with the highest incidence is Suffolk County.
  • In the death rate vs poverty and the incidence rate vs median income charts, Suffolk County is at the top end of both.
  • It shows high poverty, high median income, high death rate, and high incidence rate. This is not dissimilar to how Texas reports.
  • Cancer treatments are costly, so one might expect higher income areas to have a lower death rate, but that is not the case here.
  • The data does not indicate whether treatments occurred before death, and it is unclear whether cancer cases are disproportionately concentrated in the high-poverty population.
  • Health professionals should further dissect the data to determine who is being affected socioeconomically, because the summary chart shows that the lower the median income, the lower the death rate.

Project Summary

Challenges
  • Finding factors relevant to cancer rates without redundancy.
  • Preprocessing to clean and verify values, including removal of nulls.
Data sources
  • United States Cancer Statistics and public repositories.
  • U.S. Census population statistics.
What the dashboards provide
  • Clarity on relationships within the dataset.
  • A starting point for health professionals to decide how to approach this issue.
Key findings
  • Cancer incidence is highest in the Northeastern United States, with notable outliers such as Kentucky, Illinois, and Louisiana.
  • High-income areas are not immune to incidence and death rates, indicating that access to medical intervention does not always reduce incidences or deaths.
  • New York is surrounded by other high-incidence states and shows a distributed rate across counties.
Recommendations
  • Use these visualizations to target high-incidence areas for prevention.
  • Create more preventive healthcare opportunities at lower cost in areas with both high incidence and high poverty to mitigate death rates.
Limitations
  • The data is an amalgamation of sources and some records were incomplete.
  • The data does not provide a holistic view of diagnoses.
  • The dataset is limited in its ability to capture socioeconomic indicators that are causal rather than correlated.
